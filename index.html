Alissa Marie Doucet

Evolutionary Biologist & Entomologist

I am a PhD student in the Committee on Evolutionary Biology at the University of Chicago and the Field Museum of Natural History. I study the evolution of nocturnal insects under global change, with a special interest in nocturnal Lepidoptera.

I integrate fieldwork, museum collections, environmental DNA, automated camera traps, and deep learning to track changes in timing, range, and ecological interactions.

Outside of research, I love spending time with my wife, Taylor, and two fur kids, Smudge and Hazel Rose, reading fantasy, trying new vegan foods, and adventuring with friends.

Research

My dissertation, Life After Dark on a Changing Planet, examines how evolutionary history—both deep constraints and rapid adaptive responses—determines which moth species persist or decline under environmental change.

e.g. iNaturalist map or range data

Macroevolutionary Patterns

Analyzing trait-mediated phenological, geographic, and voltinism shifts in North American moth species using iNaturalist occurrence records.

e.g. Mothbox camera trap setup

Multi-Method Monitoring

Integrating automated camera traps, environmental DNA (eDNA), deep learning, and museum collections to develop more complete approaches for surveying nocturnal Lepidoptera communities.

e.g. moth visiting flower at night

Evolutionary Community Ecology

Investigating how evolutionary history and traits organize temporal and floral niche partitioning within a nocturnal moth community.

e.g. common garden or seasonal cue

Rapid Evolution

Testing whether contemporary moth populations show evidence of rapid evolutionary responses to shifting seasonal cues.
